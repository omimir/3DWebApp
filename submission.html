  1. Packaged project, launched from VS Code

    Vitrine is a static site with no build step. The repository includes .vscode/extensions.json, which recommends the Live Preview extension. Open index.html in VS Code, choose "Show Preview", and the app runs. It can also be served with npx serve . from the project root.

  5. Deeper understanding statement

    Custom GLSL shaders

    The globe has a thin shell mesh around it running a custom ShaderMaterial. Its fragment shader works out a fresnel term from the view direction and the surface normal: small where the surface faces the camera, large where it turns away. That value is used as the alpha of an additively blended colour, so it reads as a faint atmosphere at the globe's edge without touching the globe's own surface texture.

    There is a second piece of custom GLSL in the post-processing: a vignette pass that darkens the frame edges after tone mapping, the way a studio photo falls off towards the corners.

    Post-processing

    The viewer has an optional pipeline built with the Three.js EffectComposer: a render pass, a bloom pass on the bright highlights, tone mapping, the custom vignette, and an FXAA anti-aliasing pass. It is switched on and off from the controls, and the bloom is eased back when the visitor asks for reduced motion.

    Work beyond the lab material

    The 3D layer uses Three.js for glTF loading, orbit controls, image-based lighting, shadow mapping, raycasting, two camera types and the post-processing chain. The app is structured as model, view and controller in vanilla JavaScript modules, joined by a small publish-and-subscribe event bus, and it renders on demand so the page is idle when nothing moves.

    Each model has an animation of its own rather than a generic spin. The can's tab hinges open on a pivot at the rivet, the bottle's cap unscrews and lifts off, and the globe's sphere spins on its stand and coasts to a stop. The bottle body is also swapped to a transmissive clear-plastic material as the model loads. The models are exported from Blender to glTF, and a dependency-free Node script converts the glTF to X3D, since Blender 5.1 no longer ships an X3D exporter.

    Testing

    I checked the app as I built it: every control was tried as it was added, the browser console was watched for errors, and the network requests were confirmed. On a final pass I went through the gallery, every control, all three model animations, the exploded view, the texture swap and the post-processing together, with no console errors.
